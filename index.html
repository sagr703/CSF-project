Coupled EEG, Hemodynamics & CSF in Human Sleep

The first direct evidence that neural slow waves drive pulsatile CSF flow through coupled hemodynamic oscillations during NREM sleep.

Science 2019
Fultz, Bonmassar, Setsompop et al.
Boston University & MGH / Harvard

Coupled electrophysiological, hemodynamic, and cerebrospinal fluid oscillations in human sleep

Using simultaneous fast fMRI (TR <400ms) and EEG in 13 subjects, this study directly measured the temporal relationship between brain electrical activity, blood flow, and CSF dynamics during natural sleep.

  • Large 0.05 Hz CSF oscillations emerge specifically during NREM sleep (5.52 dB power increase)
  • CSF flow is anticorrelated with cortical gray matter BOLD signal (max R = −0.48 at 2s lag)
  • EEG slow-delta waves precede hemodynamic changes, which precede CSF inflow
  • During wakefulness, CSF flow is driven by respiratory frequency (~0.25 Hz) — a passive mechanism far less efficient than sleep-driven clearance. This highlights the critical importance of sleep for active glymphatic function.

🔑 The Monro-Kellie Mechanism

The skull is a rigid container with a fixed total volume. When neural slow waves cause cerebral blood volume to decrease, CSF must flow in to compensate — creating the macroscopic "washing" waves observed by Fultz et al. This is a direct mechanical consequence of the Monro-Kellie doctrine applied to sleep physiology.

💡 Respiratory vs. Sleep-Driven CSF Flow

During wakefulness, CSF pulsations are coupled to the respiratory cycle (~0.25 Hz), resulting in minimal net fluid transport. In contrast, NREM sleep activates a 20-fold more efficient mechanism: EEG slow waves (0.05 Hz) trigger hemodynamic oscillations that generate macroscopic CSF waves. This fundamental difference explains why sleep deprivation rapidly impairs brain waste clearance and why sleep preservation is essential for preventing neurotoxin accumulation and delirium.

Accelerated Neuroimaging Approach

Fultz et al. used simultaneous EEG and fast fMRI (TR <400 ms) in 13 healthy subjects who fell asleep in the scanner. The imaging volume was positioned so that its lower edge intersected the fourth ventricle, enabling direct measurement of CSF inflow as a bright signal from fresh, unexcited fluid entering the acquisition volume. This novel approach allowed the first simultaneous measurement of EEG, BOLD, and CSF dynamics in sleeping humans.

The EEG–CSF Coupling Chain

The study demonstrated a precise temporal sequence: (1) EEG slow-delta waves (0–0.5 Hz) appear first, reflecting synchronized neural firing during NREM sleep. (2) These are followed by a hemodynamic oscillation — a decrease in cerebral blood volume visible as a BOLD signal change. (3) Finally, CSF flows into the fourth ventricle to fill the space vacated by blood. The calculated impulse response of the CSF signal to the EEG envelope matched established hemodynamic models, confirming the causal chain: EEG → Blood → CSF.

Why This Matters

This study was the first to demonstrate that brain waste clearance (via CSF flow) is not a passive process but is actively driven by the brain's own electrical rhythms during sleep. It explains why sleep quality — specifically the depth and prevalence of slow-wave NREM sleep — is so critical for long-term brain health. Impaired slow-wave activity (e.g., in aging, sleep disorders, or critical illness) directly reduces CSF-mediated waste clearance, potentially accelerating neurodegeneration and cognitive decline.

The Glymphatic System

The Brain's Waste Clearance Highway

A brain-wide fluid transport system that operates primarily during sleep to clear neurotoxic metabolites.

CSF Entry via Periarterial Spaces

CSF enters the brain parenchyma along the spaces surrounding penetrating arteries (Virchow-Robin spaces), driven by arterial pulsatility and the sleep-driven CSF waves described by Fultz et al.

AQP4-Mediated Exchange

Astrocytic aquaporin-4 (AQP4) water channels on glial endfeet facilitate the exchange of CSF with interstitial fluid (ISF) throughout the brain parenchyma, enabling bulk flow of solutes.

Waste Clearance

Metabolic byproducts including amyloid-β, tau, and pro-inflammatory cytokines are swept from the interstitium into the CSF and transported toward perivenous drainage routes.

Meningeal Lymphatic Efflux

Waste-laden CSF exits along perivenous spaces toward meningeal lymphatic vessels and cervical lymph nodes, completing the clearance cycle.

🌙 Sleep Dependency

Glymphatic flux is 2–4× higher during sleep than wakefulness, and is further enhanced during deep NREM sleep with high slow-wave activity. This explains why even partial sleep deprivation can lead to measurable accumulation of amyloid-β in the human brain.

Mechanical Ventilation Suppresses Glymphatic Function

A murine model demonstrating that ICU-standard mechanical ventilation acutely disrupts CSF dynamics and produces delirium-like behavioral symptoms.

bioRxiv 2026
Liu, Wang, Tong, Boesen, Nedergaard, Du et al.
University of Rochester Medical Center

Mechanical Ventilation Suppresses Glymphatic Function in Parallel with Delirium-Like Symptoms in Mice

Using healthy mice subjected to 2h of mechanical ventilation with isoflurane + fentanyl (mimicking ICU protocols), the researchers measured glymphatic transport and behavioral outcomes.

  • Mechanical ventilation acutely increased intracranial pressure (ICP)
  • Long-lasting suppression of glymphatic transport (persisting after ventilation ceased)
  • Impaired brain-to-blood waste clearance and cytokine accumulation
  • Delirium-like behavior at 24h: hypolocomotion, thigmotaxis, cognitive errors
  • Nose-cone anesthesia alone (without MV) did NOT produce these effects

Glymphatic Dysfunction: A Unifying Hypothesis for Delirium

A comprehensive framework proposing that impaired glymphatic clearance is the central mechanism through which diverse risk factors converge to produce delirium.

Nat Rev Neurol 2026
Boesen, Du, Goldman, Nedergaard et al.
Zealand University Hospital & University of Rochester

Glymphatic dysfunction: a unifying hypothesis for delirium

Published in Nature Reviews Neurology, this Perspective article synthesizes evidence from multiple fields to propose glymphatic failure as the common pathway through which all major delirium risk factors and triggers act.

  • Delirium affects 20–50% of ICU patients; up to 80% of ventilated patients
  • All major risk factors (aging, dementia, CVD, renal failure) reduce glymphatic clearance
  • All major triggers (infection, surgery, sleep deprivation) impair glymphatic function
  • ICU interventions (sedation, opioids, noradrenaline) suppress glymphatic flux
  • Therapeutic target: preserving/enhancing glymphatic clearance in critically ill patients

📊 Fultz 2019 Contribution

Established the mechanistic foundation: EEG slow waves during NREM sleep are the upstream driver of CSF flow. Without slow-wave activity, the entire glymphatic cascade is impaired. This explains why EEG is a biomarker for glymphatic function.

🐭 Liu 2026 Contribution

Demonstrated causality in a translational model: mechanical ventilation — a common ICU intervention — is sufficient on its own to suppress glymphatic function and produce delirium-like symptoms, establishing the mechanistic link between ICU care and delirium.

🔬 Boesen 2026 Contribution

Provided the unifying clinical framework: glymphatic dysfunction is not just one pathway to delirium but the central convergence point for all known risk factors and triggers, making it the most promising therapeutic target for delirium prevention.

Therapeutic Strategies by Mechanism

Potential interventions targeting different points in the sleep–CSF–delirium pathway

💊 Pharmacological Targets

Agents that enhance glymphatic flux (e.g., AQP4 modulators), reduce ICP, or preserve sleep architecture may reduce delirium incidence. Avoiding opioids and benzodiazepines — known glymphatic suppressors — is a priority.

🛌 Sleep Preservation Protocols

Protecting slow-wave sleep in ICU patients through noise reduction, circadian rhythm maintenance, and sleep-promoting approaches could maintain glymphatic function and reduce delirium risk. Tui therapy may potentially be combined with other interventions to support natural sleep architecture and preserve EEG slow-wave activity.

🫁 Ventilation Optimization

Liu et al.'s findings suggest that ventilation strategies minimizing ICP elevation — such as lower tidal volumes, optimized PEEP, and head positioning — may preserve glymphatic function and reduce delirium.

🌙 Preserving Sleep Architecture in the ICU

Sleep preservation is critical for maintaining the EEG-hemodynamic-CSF coupling mechanism that drives glymphatic clearance. Non-pharmacological approaches, potentially including Tui therapy in combination with other sleep-promoting interventions, may help preserve natural sleep architecture and circadian rhythm integrity in ICU settings. Such integrated approaches could address the root cause of delirium identified by Fultz, Liu, and Boesen: the failure of the brain's waste-clearance system. Further research is needed to evaluate whether combined sleep preservation strategies can reduce neurotoxin accumulation and delirium incidence in critically ill patients.

🔮 Future Research Directions

The convergence of these three studies points toward several critical research needs: (1) Development of non-invasive biomarkers for glymphatic function in ICU patients (e.g., EEG-based indices of slow-wave activity); (2) Clinical trials testing glymphatic-enhancing interventions for delirium prevention, including combined sleep preservation approaches; (3) Investigation of whether post-delirium cognitive impairment reflects persistent glymphatic dysfunction; (4) Exploration of whether the EEG–CSF coupling mechanism identified by Fultz et al. can be monitored in real-time to guide ICU care and intervention optimization.
